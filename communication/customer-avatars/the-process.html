1. To tailor-make our community initiatives to maximize impact:
    1. Passionate individuals
    2. Excitement about our mission and vision
  2. Who is our market? Do we really know? Have we truly assessed our consumer motivations and expectations?
  3. How should these constituencies be best served? Have we mobilized the best data and ideas from our community?
  4. Invite users to regular 1-on-1’s and group “feedback/get to know the team” calls
    1. Request them to go through the charter beforehand
    2. Ask each person to share a little story about who they are and why they’re here:
      1. What are the things that motivate you?
      2. Why do you get out of bed each morning?
      3. What are they here on this earth to do?
    3. Keep asking questions in order to understand their entire “user journey”
      1. How did you find us?
      2. What is your current perception of what we are doing?
      3. What do you think we should do differently?
        1. Ask them to get specific. This is a safe space.
      4. How can you help achieve our mission?
    4. Ask, “How can we help you?”
      1. With the expectation that not all requests will be fulfilled
    5. Drill down on specific questions that relate to future features
      1. Get our users to imagine, explain, or test real-life scenarios about the product
    6. Get them to take action
      1. Define next steps
        1. E.g. giving them a task to get started
      2. Ask them to fill out the Notion form if they haven't
      3. Assign them to a lab/s
    7. Our community might have multiple different “categories” of users who share similar motivations, goals, and frustrations/problems
  5. It's a great starting point, but customer personas are no longer enough on its own.
  6. Once we know the demographics, psychographics, and other details that a typical persona provides, we need to keep digging.
    1. The goal now is to figure out how each potential customer thinks and what they value.
  7. Each person that fits a particular customer persona might share similarities.
    1. But numerous variables come into play that results in important differences — for example, industry.
  8. Let's say our top persona is a female executive. In a purchasing role, between age 30 and 40, with a $100,000 median income.
    1. If that woman is in the manufacturing industry, she's going to have drastically different priorities from those of the same persona in the tech industry.
  9. Furthermore, we should consider the organizational size.
    1. Even if two target buyers fit the same persona and are in the same industry, they still don't necessarily value the same things.
    2. For instance, one might be in a tech startup, whereas the other is in an enterprise software company.
      1. Their worlds are millions of miles apart, and they'll need different content that speaks to different issues.
  10. There are other variables in addition to those (e.g., geography) that affect how personas think and what they value.
    1. It's our job to get as nuanced and specific as possible, based on all of those criteria, before trying to create and share the right content.
  11. Go through and tag every single piece of content based on persona, stage of the buyer journey, industry, company size, geography, and so forth.
  12. By the time we're done, we should be able to quickly pull a deeply relevant, personalized piece of content for just about anyone.
